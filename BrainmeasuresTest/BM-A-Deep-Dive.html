Ten Pointers for Creating Video-Based Instruction

With the abrupt shift from traditional workspaces to home offices, the requirement to train today's business personnel did not go down but rather increased. Individuals can no longer ask a coworker two chairs over for help when they need it or simply meet in person to discuss experiences. While online communication can be beneficial, it is virtually always more time-consuming and inefficient than in-person conversation.

Brainmeasures must, therefore, now take on the task of offering high-quality learning opportunities that are customized to meet the demands of the "new normal."

One practical option for providing urgently required instruction is video learning. Virtually anyone can use the technology, and there are plenty of online tutorials accessible.

However, education has unique requirements, so there are a few things to think about before starting to make instructional videos. Now, let's examine ten design principles for video-based training:

Create a solid storyboard first, then follow it through:

Because you are not trying to create a beautiful product but rather an ordinary andpractical one, planning is essential. Both the learning objectives and the strategiesfor achieving them are quite pertinent. The student must be the focus of the video rather than the filmmaker's artistic abilities.

A thorough storyboard will guarantee that every learning resource is included inthe presentation and provide an overview of the final design. Describing eachperson's role in the process will also benefit everyone involved in the design andexecution phases.

The storyboard must include all relevant explanations because it will be used bymultiple people with different backgrounds, much like a blueprint for a newconstruction.

Before you begin, gather all the tools you'll need and locate the appropriate individuals:

Making a list of all the personnel, objects, and equipment that will be neededshould be simple, thanks to the storyboard. Prior to starting to construct the video,all of these logistical issues need to be taken care of.The "we'll cross that bridge when we get there" mentality will almost always resultin inventions that turn out to be very expensive, particularly in terms of quality.Today's audiences virtually consider watching videos to be a hallowed pastime.Therefore, poor quality will either make them stop watching altogether, growdisinterested, or even provide pretty hurtful criticism.The video learning material must be excellent, if not perfect, in order to captivateand teach viewers.

Publish instructional videos on several platforms:

Adult learners of today agree that employing video content to aid in the transfer ofknowledge is beneficial, but they differ in their preferences about the location andmethod of material accessibility.While some people utilize laptops or PCs, others would rather download themodules and study them offline. There are differences in the devices themselves aswell as the operating systems that follow.Therefore, you have two choices when creating an instructional film that needs tobe seen by a wide range of people. One option is to go with a flexible platform thatenables material to be displayed across a range of devices. The alternative is tohost your films from an updated LMS with mobile capabilities, which is safer inthe event of large organizations.

Remember that size counts

Here, there are two things to talk about. First, there is an undeniable trend towardslearning in bite-sized chunks. Even with their engaging qualities, videos that go onfor too long might get boring. Consumers want to locate the precise informationthey require with the least amount of supporting information.

The actual size of the video (measured in data transmission units) is another crucialconsideration. It may need help loading, freezing, and occasionally buffering if it istoo big, making for a better user experience and even worse information retentionrates.In light of this, make an effort to optimize the video's size and substance (a safetyinstructions course, for instance, does not require recording and rendering in 4K).After it's finished, take another look to see if there are any changes that may bemade.

When necessary, update the materials:

It does not follow that a certain video-learning unit is automatically included in theevergreen material section just because it was excellent when it first "aired."Numbers are regularly changed, new information is constantly emerging, andstudies occasionally demonstrate that a large portion of what we once believed tobe unchangeable has changed drastically.If there are just small revisions, adding to the video module is OK, but if moreinformation becomes outdated, it is preferable to start over from scratch.Using the same storyboard outline is acceptable as long as the information isinteresting and new to the eye and ears. At best, the film is a historicaldocumentary if it discusses the newest developments in gadget connectivity butstill uses dial-up internet.

Give the Face a Name:

Recall the last occasion you spent watching the news. Why, in your opinion, arejournalists using cameras to report news stories? Humans are socially wired tofocus on other people. Having your instructor's face on screen is crucial if youwant to establish a stronger connection with your students. A face on the screenfosters concentration, builds confidence, and shows that the instructor is involved.It's easy to illustrate. Though persuading your teachers to go on camera canoccasionally be challenging, the results are well worth the effort.

Engage Your Learners in Conversation:

It's time to use your LMS to engage your stakeholders, enable effective usage ofyour workflows, relieve some strain on your SLA, and support the release tempo ofyour final product once the instructor's face is on the screen and they are preparedto deliver content.

Do you see what I did there? If you're anything like me, the second part of the lineescaped your notice. You must converse with a wide range of learners in aconversational manner when you are training them.

One popular argument against this idea is that since training is designed forindividuals who are already familiar with the jargon of their field, they willunderstand. For that reason alone, especially if you're expecting them to pushthrough technical training material, take a break from the routine and talk to theminformally. You might be surprised at how grateful the experts are if you try tomake complex ideas simple to the general public.

They Won't Watch It If They Can't Hear It

When it comes to video, audio is far too frequently overlooked. Some remark,"Don't worry, the built-in microphone should be good enough." "All we need to dois record the voice with our phones and sync it to the video later."It makes no difference how brilliant your camera is or how amazing your videocontent looks on screen if the learners are unable to hear you well or if there isbackground noise that is distracting. Students will go.Audio quality problems have no justification in the present era of high-quality,inexpensive USB microphones that you can plug straight into your computer. Aclear, full-toned teacher voice will complement the hard work you put into yourexcellent video content if you use a good USB microphone in a quiet environment.

Use 1080 pixels or higher while recording videos

The majority of your students will be viewing your movies on PC screens with1080p or greater resolutions. The video resolution you are recording should becarefully considered. When a speaker is on stage, and the video is a little fuzzy,learners can overlook it, but if you show them a screen grab of a user interface withtext on it that is too grainy to read, you risk losing their attention.In a similar line, it's crucial to remember that just because the text appears readablein a tiny embedded frame on a website or learning management system (LMS), itdoesn't necessarily translate well when blown up to fill the screen on the majorityof displays. Examine your material in both portrait and landscape mode.

The kitten's attention span is here:

We have collectively lost the attention span necessary to sit through a two-hourrecorded lecture in the age of YouTube and Instagram, even when the topic issomething we are enthusiastic about. We'll play with another toy, just like kitties.

Keep your movies brief and to the point if you want to keep your students' interest.Avoid the temptation to write an hour-long lesson on a single technical topic.Videos should last five to ten minutes. Attempt to maintain movement on thescreen as well. Make careful to animate the slide points in the slideshow programor in a post-production suite if you're using static slides. By doing this, you willbring attention to the main idea and enhance the watching experience.

Remember that the real material you provide in your courses is really important,even if you become bogged down in the details of your learning systems andcourse design. Thankfully, there are easy methods to make it stand out andmaintain the interest and support of your students.

The Reasons for Incorporating Video into Your Online Classes

These days, everyone uses videos for a variety of purposes. Businesses use it to advertise their goods, and nonprofits use it to disseminate their message of compassion. Adding video content to your training modules and e-learning courses has become increasingly beneficial. Videos are ideal for providing learners with a personalized learning path because of their capacity to compress large amounts of information into brief segments.

Because of this, videos are now a fantastic addition to written manuals and online courses. For this reason, a lot of e-learning producers collaborate closely with instructional video firms to create content for their courses. But are films really such a helpful teaching tool because of their shortness? More importantly, how can you make use of them?

Acquiring knowledge while on the fly

Currently, the biggest obstacle in online training and education is adjusting to thedaily schedules of learners. E-learning courses are a solution that enables people toactively engage in their education as they go about their lives, as we only have somuch time in a day.

A practical way to engage students and quicken their learning is through video.With the video's lively and fast-paced structure, you may use it to entice viewerseven more.

Since most audiences have limited time and attention spans, the best instructivevideos are typically concise and to the point.Videos are entertaining to watch; therefore, they don't interfere with education.

Conversely, people always remember an amazing video!Short lesson units are typically used to organize video-based courses. Studentswould still rather review a lesson in its entirety in one sitting or have it divided intodigestible, smaller portions, even if they are pressed for time.

Videos, however, are more than just extended lectures. Their ability to tell a taleeffectively will determine how successful they are.

Drawing lessons from narratives

Though not to me, stories like Cinderella and The Little Prince may appearridiculous to adults these days. Even now, years after we originally read orwatched them, we can still clearly recall the lessons they imparted to us. For whatreason is that the case?

Because it gives timeless themes life, a well-crafted story has greater persuasivepower than any master class or textbook. When we lose ourselves in a story, webecome aware of a subject's real-world significance rather than dealing withunclear, abstract ideas.Whether your course consists of professional training modules or sophisticatedscience, your instructional films should, to the greatest extent feasible, usecompelling narratives to reinforce the concepts being taught. Users are betterequipped to apply their knowledge to specific tasks when they are provided with anengaging story about how an issue develops in real life.

Acquiring knowledge by observing the behavior of others

As I previously stated, an online course is only complete if it demonstrates tostudents how to apply the theory to actual situations.

In actuality, you can instruct consumers on how to, for example, lessen theircarbon footprint or adhere to a convoluted, step-by-step business strategy.However, they will barely have a realistic idea of what it looks like to complete thework on their own if they don't witness someone else doing it.

The stakes increase dramatically in your instructional videos when you incorporatecharacters. Through witnessing others who share their struggles, students cancomprehensively understand how to overcome their obstacles.You want people to empathize with your characters. To achieve this, you need tomake sure that your characters have personalities. Regardless of how brief yourvideo is, you should always give your story's protagonists names anddistinguishing characteristics.All that's left to do is emotionally connect with your audience when your videosare packed with plot, characters, and storytelling!

Playing on the emotions of the audience

Retention of knowledge is dependent on emotions and values. Ensuring your videos can emotionally connect with your learners is crucial when creating e- learning content.

A strong video screenplay should impact viewers deeply and encourage them to continue their education on their own rather than making them cry. Your e-learning videos can be emotionally charged in a number of ways:

Turn education into an adventure. Your characters' journey must involve more than just finding the solution; you also want to show them overcoming challenges.

Play some music to help your composition come to life. Instead of existing on its own, music should be used as an addition to heighten the impact of your video's message.

Final remarks

You must use videos in your e-learning courses. Like no other kind of information,they have the power to inspire individuals to participate in their education whenused appropriately and actively.

Videos, on the one hand, provide easily digestible content that people may watchwhenever they choose without interfering with their daily schedules. On the otherhand, when vibrant stories and characters are paired with videos, a specialemotional incentive for learning is produced.

Videos are a fantastic and original choice for your e-learning courses for all ofthese reasons and more.
